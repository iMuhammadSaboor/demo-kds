What this fixes

Paper dockets are killing our rush.

Three real things that happen every week, and how this KDS addresses each.

Today

Tickets get buried. The printer spits a docket, it lands under two others, by the time the cook sees it we're ten minutes behind. Customer's stood there since minute one.

With the KDS

Every order is always on screen. Oldest on top. Red border + pulse the moment it passes its prep target. No docket ever gets forgotten — there is no docket.

Today

No one knows how late "late" is. The cook guesses. The customer asks twice. Sometimes you make a second one because the first got lost in the pile.

With the KDS

Per-ticket timer, colour-coded by prep target. A 12-min brekkie isn't "late" at 4 minutes — a 3-min flat white is. The screen knows the difference.

Today

Nothing gets logged. When a ticket's made, docket goes in the bin. We have zero data on prep times, busy hours, what gets re-made.

With the KDS

Every bump is timestamped. The history page shows avg prep time, on-time rate, orders by hour, top items. First week you'll know exactly when to pull a second barista on.

How it works

A tablet on the pass. A laptop at the counter. Done.

No new hardware beyond one tablet. The counter fires orders from any device with a browser.

Mount a tablet on the pass.

Android, iPad, anything with a browser. Open /kds.html full-screen. That's it — no app install.

Open the counter terminal on the POS laptop.

Tap items by category, pick mods (oat, decaf, no onion), add a customer name, tap Send to Kitchen. Ticket pops on the tablet with a chime.

Cook bumps when done.

Tap the green bump bar, or press the hotkey number on a Bluetooth keypad. Tile animates out. Order moves to history with its prep time logged.

Review the day on the history page.

KPIs, by-hour chart, channel mix, prep-time distribution, bumped-order log. Everything the old paper system never captured.

Later: wire it to Square / Uber / DoorDash.

The queue is a simple JSON log — Square's order webhook pushes into it, Uber Eats webhook pushes into it, the cafe's own online page POSTs into it. One screen, every channel.

Why I built this

Built by someone who's worked the rush.

Paper ticket systems are fine for 10 orders in a morning — they fall over at 40. Commercial KDS products cost $25/month per device and still don't fit how a small cafe actually works. I wanted to show what a KDS actually looks like, running on hardware a cafe already owns, tailored to a real kitchen.

This is a working prototype, not a pitch deck. Open it. Use it. Break it. It speaks Square's real order webhooks out of the box — sandbox today, production tomorrow.

Under the hood

Boring stack, on purpose.

Built with the idea that a cafe owner shouldn't need a DevOps engineer to keep it running.

Frontend
Vanilla HTML + JS
No framework. No build step. Runs in any browser.
Server
Zero-dep Node
One file, 70 lines. node serve.mjs.
Data
localStorage + JSON log
Swap for Postgres at go-live. Schema already matches.
Cross-tab sync
storage events
Counter → KDS in the same browser instantly. WebSocket later.
Sound
Web Audio API
No mp3 file needed. Two-tone chime, software-generated.
Charts
Chart.js 4
One CDN link. Pin to local at go-live.
Typography
Space Grotesk + Inter
Google Fonts. Mono for data.
Total size
~48 KB uncompressed
Loads on any kitchen wifi, instantly.
